Chipless RFID: low-cost tags & localisation

B.Tech. Project · IIIT-Delhi · Advisor: Dr. Mohammad Hashmi · Best B.Tech. Project

A fully passive RFID tag with no chip and no power supply — and a way to figure out where it is.

Conventional RFID tags rely on a silicon chip, and that chip is most of the cost. A chipless tag removes it entirely: the tag is a passive structure with no chip and no battery, which makes it far cheaper to produce. In my design, the identifying code is encoded as a series of impedance discontinuities along a matched transmission line. The reader sends a short pulse modulated at 2.45 GHz; each discontinuity reflects part of that pulse back, and the timing and amplitude of those reflections spell out the tag's bit pattern. I designed, optimized, and simulated the tag across test cases in Keysight ADS, and also explored a second encoding scheme using pulse-position modulation with group-delay-engineered microwave C-sections, which needs no external components.

The second half of the project tackled localisation: once you can read a tag, can you tell where it is? I studied an Inverse Synthetic Aperture Radar (ISAR) approach that uses the phase of arrival and the phase difference of arrival of the reflected signal to estimate the position of a moving tag.

The work was recognized as the Best B.Tech. Project in my graduating batch.
