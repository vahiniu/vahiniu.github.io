Direction-of-arrival estimation in realistic radar

M.S. thesis · The Ohio State University · 2019

How well do radar direction-finding algorithms actually hold up once you add the messiness of the real world?

Direction-of-arrival (DoA) estimation — working out the angle a signal is coming from — is one of the core tasks in radar. Most published comparisons, though, evaluate these algorithms in idealized conditions. My thesis asked how they behave under realistic impairments: multipath reflections (modeled geometrically off one and two walls), additive white Gaussian noise, and the quantization error that comes from finite angular resolution.

I modeled the radar array signal with these effects and compared three DoA approaches across a range of signal-to-noise ratios and multipath parameters: the matched filter, the MUSIC subspace algorithm, and basis-pursuit denoising (an L1-norm, compressed-sensing formulation solved with SPGL1). To ground the comparison, I used the Cramér–Rao lower bound as the theoretical best-case benchmark for estimation error.

Finally, I explored a machine-learning angle: a denoising autoencoder trained to map interference-corrupted radar measurements back toward their clean, interference-free counterparts. Feeding the denoised signals back into the DoA estimators let me measure how much a learned denoiser could recover the accuracy lost to multipath and noise.
