Non-invasive glucose sensing

Side project · multi-sensor hardware + signal processing

Could you estimate blood glucose without a finger prick — frequently, and cheaply? A side project exploring non-invasive, multimodal glucose sensing.

Managing diabetes usually means pricking a finger and reading a drop of blood on a strip — accurate, but painful enough that people don't do it often. Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) solve the frequency problem but are expensive and reach only a small slice of the population. The goal of this side project was to explore a non-invasive, low-cost alternative someone could use as often as they like — eventually from a phone paired with a simple wearable — and, along the way, to look for a correlation between glucose and galvanic skin response.

The setup combined optical and electrical sensing: a PPG (photoplethysmography) pulse-oximeter, a GSR (galvanic skin response) sensor, and a temperature sensor, read by an Arduino. The premise is that glucose in the interstitial fluid leaves a faint signature across these biomarkers — for instance, glucose subtly changes how near-infrared light is absorbed in blood. We used signal processing (filtering, denoising, feature extraction) to clean the raw signals, then trained a deep-learning model to map the combined features to a glucose estimate. A table-top fixture kept the sensors steady to cut motion noise.

How it was validated: the model was trained on data we collected with our own setup, using a Freestyle Libre 3 continuous glucose monitor as the ground truth. Each reading was timestamped and paired with a short journal note — workout, meal, stress, mood — so the dataset captured real glucose swings. A small pilot also chased a side question: can galvanic skin response on its own flag rising glucose? (Prior work has reported correlations as high as ~94%.)
