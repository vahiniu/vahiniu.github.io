Non-invasive glucose sensing

Side project · multi-sensor hardware + signal processing

Could you estimate blood glucose without pricking a finger? A weekend hardware experiment to find out.

Standard glucometers need a blood sample. For this side project we built a small wearable sensor rig to explore a fully non-invasive alternative, following a multi-sensor approach described by Mahmud, Limon, Ahmed, and colleagues.

The architecture combined three skin-surface signals: a PPG (photoplethysmography) pulse-oximeter that shines red, green, and near-infrared light through the skin and measures how blood flow changes the reflected light; a GSR (galvanic skin response) sensor that measures skin conductance; and a temperature sensor. An Arduino read all three sensors and streamed the data out for processing, where the combined signals could be mapped to an estimated glucose level and sanity-checked against a conventional glucometer.

The idea: glucose subtly changes how near-infrared light is absorbed in blood, so a model trained on these multi-sensor signals can learn that signature and predict glucose without drawing blood. This was a side project — a chance to wire up biosignal hardware and play with the signal processing end to end.

How it was validated: the model was trained on validated data collected with our own setup, using a Freestyle Libre 3 continuous glucose monitor as the ground truth.
